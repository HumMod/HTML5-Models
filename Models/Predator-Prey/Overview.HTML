Overview

This is the Predator-Prey Model. Wolves are the predators and moose are the prey.

This model starts with some ordinary population dynamics ... and then it gets interesting.

The moose herd size (head count) is the integral over time of the moose birth rate minus the moose death rate.

The wolf herd size (head count) is the integral over time of the wolf birth rate minus the wolf death rate.

Birth rates are the product of a fertility factor and herd size.

Death rates are the product of a mortality factor and herd size.

Now here is where it gets interesting. We add in predation. Wolves kill the moose and eat them. This has two important effects.

  • Predation increases wolf fertility as a function of moose food value, wolf hunting skills, and the size of the wolf herd.

  • Predation increases moose mortality as a function of wolf hunting skills and the size of the wolf herd.
So the end result is that while wolves are increasing their fertility factor, they are also decreasing their food supply. The model illustrates that this is a very unstable relationship.
